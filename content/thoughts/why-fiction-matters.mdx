Most of the things I genuinely understand about history, politics, religion, power, and people (not just know, but actually understand) didn't come from textbooks or structured reading or carefully curated non-fiction. They came from novels.

I've been sitting with that observation for a while now, turning it over, trying to figure out why it's true.

Part of it, I think, is that fiction does something quite specific that other forms of writing rarely can. It makes knowledge feel lived rather than explained. There's a difference between being told what something was like and being made to feel it from the inside. Non-fiction, even very good non-fiction, tends to work from the outside in. Fiction works the other way.

When I read Ken Follett's Century Trilogy, something shifted in how I understood the two world wars. Not because the book was more accurate than a history textbook (it wasn't trying to be) but because it was personal in a way that history books almost never are. You move through those decades alongside families, friendships, political movements, ordinary lives, and slowly you start to understand that nobody living through those years experienced them as history. Nobody woke up thinking they were participating in a globally significant geopolitical transition. People were surviving, loving, working, protecting their families, trying to make sense of chaos while enormous forces moved through the world largely beyond their control.

That understanding, that history is not a sequence of events but a weather system that real people have to live inside, is something I don't think I could have arrived at any other way. A date disappears from memory. A character doesn't.

The Glass Palace by Amitav Ghosh did something similar but quieter. It moves through colonialism, migration, identity, and memory across generations in a way that's almost impossible to describe structurally because the book doesn't really announce its intentions. It just accumulates. By the end you haven't just learned what empire does politically. You understand what it does emotionally, privately, in the way families carry displacement across decades without naming it. That kind of understanding is hard to come by. It requires a story long enough and patient enough to show you something revealing itself slowly over time.

And then there's Shogun, which honestly felt less like reading a novel and more like entering a civilization.

What James Clavell does brilliantly is force the reader to experience confusion before understanding. You arrive in feudal Japan alongside Blackthorne, and everything is strange: the customs, the power structures, the silences, the religious tensions, the way people communicate and don't communicate. You're not given a neat explanatory framework. You're made to be a foreigner first. And then gradually, as the story unfolds, the internal logic of that world begins to reveal itself. Things that seemed arbitrary start making sense. You begin to understand not just what that society believed but why those beliefs made sense to the people living inside them, which is such a different thing, and so much harder to arrive at.

That's one of fiction's particular strengths. It teaches understanding before judgment. It slows down the part of you that wants to evaluate from a distance and asks you to inhabit first.

कितने पाकिस्तान by Kamleshwar does something even more ambitious. It's a book that's hard to summarize because it almost dissolves the boundary between literature, history, philosophy, and moral inquiry. It reads like a conversation happening across centuries, between civilizations, religions, borders, rulers, victims, ideas. Reading it, you start asking questions that extend far beyond the subcontinent: why do humans divide themselves so reliably and so violently along lines they themselves invented? Why does identity so easily become the permission for cruelty? Why do nations keep rehearsing old catastrophes while genuinely believing they've moved past them?

The book doesn't hand you answers. It hands you better questions, which I think is the more generous thing to do.

---

That quality, the ability to generate curiosity rather than just transfer information, might be what I love most about serious fiction. A great novel sends you outward. You finish reading and suddenly you're searching for maps, pulling up historical events, following philosophical ideas down long corridors you didn't know existed. One good story can set off an entire chain reaction of learning that no syllabus could have planned. The book becomes a door rather than a destination.

There's something deeply old about this. Long before formal education, before textbooks and curricula and standardized tests, human beings understood the world through stories. Myths, epics, oral traditions, religious narratives, folklore. Stories were never just entertainment. They were how civilizations stored memory, values, survival knowledge, moral complexity, fear, and identity. The Mahabharata isn't just a poem. It's a portable civilization. The Iliad is, among other things, a meditation on what glory costs. These weren't marginal cultural artefacts. They were how people made sense of being alive.

In some ways, I don't think that has changed as much as we pretend.

What fiction offers, at its best, is something that feels increasingly rare: genuine slowness. Not the slow of waiting for something to happen, but the slow of sitting inside a world long enough to understand it. You spend weeks with characters who don't exist and come away understanding real people better than before. You inhabit a worldview completely unlike your own for hundreds of pages and emerge from it slightly less certain that your own assumptions are the only reasonable ones. You encounter contradiction, characters who are intelligent and self-destructive, civilizations that are brilliant and brutal, progress and violence growing in the same soil, and you're not asked to resolve it. Just to hold it.

That kind of reading feels expansive in a way that's hard to explain to someone who hasn't experienced it. Not the satisfaction of having learned something you can repeat or apply. More like the quiet feeling of having become, very slightly, a larger person than you were before you started.

<p className="closing">I keep coming back to that. Fiction at its best doesn't make you smarter in any measurable way. It makes the world feel more inhabitable. More layered. More worth paying attention to.</p>

<p className="closing">Which is, I think, what the best kind of learning has always felt like.</p>
