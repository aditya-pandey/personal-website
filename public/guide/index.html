Chapter 01 — The Foundation

Numbers, Symbols,
and Why Abstraction Matters

Before electricity, before silicon, before software — humanity had one urgent civilizational problem: how do we represent, store, and manipulate quantities? The solution they found laid the foundation for every computer ever built.

~30,000 BCE
Tally Marks
The Ishango bone (found in Congo) shows notches carved in groups — possibly the earliest evidence of counting. One mark = one thing. No symbols, no place value, just raw correspondence.
~3000 BCE
Sumerian Cuneiform Numbers
The Sumerians developed a base-60 (sexagesimal) system — which is why we still have 60 seconds in a minute and 360 degrees in a circle. Symbols pressed into clay tablets.
~200 BCE
Indian Decimal System
Indian mathematicians developed the base-10 place-value system and, crucially, the concept of zero as a number — not just an absence. This single invention made all advanced mathematics possible.
~820 CE
Al-Khwarizmi spreads the system
The Persian mathematician wrote "Al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr" — the origin of the word "algebra." His work spread Indian numerals to the Arab world, and then Europe. "Algorithm" comes from his name.
1847
George Boole — The Algebra of Logic
Boole published "The Mathematical Analysis of Logic," proving that logical reasoning could be expressed as algebra using only 0 and 1. This became the direct mathematical foundation of all digital computing.

Chapter 02 — The Language of Machines

Binary:
Everything is 0s and 1s

Computers use binary not because engineers are sadistic, but because physical systems naturally have two stable states: a switch is on or off, a capacitor is charged or uncharged, voltage is high or low. Binary maps perfectly onto this physical reality.

🔌
Physical Reality
Voltage States

Modern logic uses ~0V for "0" and ~3.3V or 5V for "1". Detecting two states is far more reliable than detecting ten — noise margins are huge.

🧮
Mathematical Power
Boolean Algebra

George Boole's 1847 system proved that all logical reasoning — AND, OR, NOT — can be expressed with just two symbols. Shannon applied this to circuits in 1937.

📦
Universality
Everything Becomes Bits

Text, images, audio, video, code, 3D models — all ultimately reduce to sequences of 1s and 0s. This universality is what makes general-purpose computers possible.

Chapter 03 — The Building Blocks of Computation

Logic Gates:
Where Math Meets Hardware

Logic gates are the fundamental units of all digital circuits. Each gate takes binary inputs and produces a binary output according to a logical rule. By combining millions of gates, you can build anything from a calculator to an AI chip.

Key Insight · 1937
Claude Shannon's Thesis

Claude Shannon's MIT master's thesis proved that Boolean algebra — the math of TRUE/FALSE — could be implemented with electrical relay circuits. This single 69-page paper is called "possibly the most important master's thesis of the 20th century."

Universal Gates
NAND is All You Need

The NAND gate (Not-AND) is "functionally complete" — any logic circuit can be built from NAND gates alone. Modern CPUs with billions of gates ultimately reduce to combinations of this single operation.

▸ How do gates actually add numbers? ▾

A "half adder" combines an XOR gate (for the sum bit) and an AND gate (for the carry bit) to add two single bits. A "full adder" adds two bits plus a carry-in, using two half adders and an OR gate. Chain 64 full adders together and you have a 64-bit adder — the kind inside every modern CPU.

Every arithmetic operation (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) decomposes into gate-level operations. Division is addition of negative numbers. Multiplication is repeated addition. It's gates all the way down.

▸ What is a flip-flop? (How computers remember things) ▾

A flip-flop is a circuit made from two cross-coupled NAND gates that can hold one bit of information — it "remembers" its state even after inputs change. This is the basis of all RAM (memory) and CPU registers.

Your computer's RAM is essentially billions of flip-flops. When you open a program, its code is loaded from storage into flip-flops in RAM, and the CPU reads from them at extreme speed. When power is cut, the flip-flops lose their state — that's why RAM is "volatile."

Chapter 04 — The Tiny Switch

Transistors & Chips:
The Revolution in Silicon

Without transistors: no computers, no internet, no smartphones, no AI. Nothing. The transistor is arguably the most important invention of the 20th century — yet most people couldn't describe what it does.

👩‍💻
Ada Lovelace
1815–1852
Often called the first programmer — she wrote an algorithm for Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine (a mechanical computer that was never built). She realized machines could manipulate symbols, not just numbers, predicting software before electricity-powered computers existed.
🧠
Alan Turing
1912–1954
Defined the theoretical "universal computing machine" (Turing Machine) in 1936, proving that a single machine with the right program could compute anything computable. This became the theoretical model for every real computer ever built.
⚡
Shockley, Bardeen & Brattain
Bell Labs, 1947
Invented the transistor at Bell Labs. It replaced vacuum tubes with a tiny semiconductor device that could amplify or switch electrical signals. They shared the 1956 Nobel Prize in Physics. Their invention made modern computing possible.
📈
Gordon Moore
1929–2023
Intel co-founder who observed in 1965 that transistor counts on chips doubled roughly every two years. "Moore's Law" held for over 50 years and is the reason your phone is billions of times more powerful than 1960s mainframes.
🔬
Apple's M3 Max chip (2023) contains 92 billion transistors in an area about the size of a thumbnail. Each transistor is approximately 3 nanometers wide — smaller than most proteins in your body. They switch on/off at speeds measured in picoseconds (trillionths of a second).

Chapter 05 — The Brain

How CPUs Work:
The Fetch-Decode-Execute Cycle

A CPU appears magical but follows a relentlessly simple loop billions of times per second: get the next instruction, figure out what it means, carry it out, store the result. Everything your computer has ever done reduces to this loop.

⚡
ALU
Arithmetic Logic Unit

The ALU performs all mathematical and logical operations — addition, subtraction, AND, OR, comparison. Modern CPUs have multiple ALUs running in parallel (superscalar execution).

📋
Registers
Ultra-Fast Scratch Space

Registers are tiny, extremely fast memory locations inside the CPU itself — access time under 1 nanosecond. A modern CPU has ~16-32 general-purpose registers, each 64 bits wide.

🗄️
Cache Hierarchy
L1 → L2 → L3 → RAM

L1 cache: ~4 cycles access. L2: ~12 cycles. L3: ~40 cycles. RAM: ~200+ cycles. Cache misses are the biggest performance bottleneck in modern computing.

▸ What is "clock speed" really measuring? ▾

Clock speed (measured in GHz) is how many times per second the CPU's internal clock ticks. Each tick is an opportunity to complete one stage of the pipeline. At 3 GHz, the clock ticks 3 billion times per second. But a modern CPU is pipelined — it overlaps multiple instructions simultaneously, doing Fetch for instruction N+1 while Executing instruction N. A modern chip might complete 3–5 instructions per clock cycle (Instructions Per Cycle, or IPC).

This is why comparing CPUs purely by GHz is misleading. Architecture matters enormously — Apple's M-series chips execute more instructions per cycle than Intel chips at similar clock speeds, which is why they're so efficient despite lower GHz numbers.

▸ How does a GPU differ from a CPU? ▾

A CPU has a few very powerful, complex cores (typically 8–32) optimized for sequential tasks with lots of branching logic. A GPU has thousands of smaller, simpler cores optimized for doing the same operation on many data points simultaneously (SIMD — Single Instruction, Multiple Data).

Rendering pixels, physics simulation, and AI training all involve doing the same math on millions of data points at once — perfect for GPUs. Training GPT-4 required tens of thousands of NVIDIA A100 GPUs running for months. The reason AI exploded after 2012 is largely because researchers realized GPUs could train neural networks 10–50× faster than CPUs.

Chapter 06 — Connected

The Internet:
Packets, Protocols, and Routing

The internet isn't a single network — it's a network of networks, all agreeing to speak the same language (TCP/IP). Its genius is that it was designed to survive nuclear attack: no single point of failure, messages self-route around damage.

📦
Layer 3 · IP
Internet Protocol

IP handles addressing and routing. Every device gets an IP address (like 192.168.1.1). Routers read destination addresses and forward packets toward their destination — each router only knows the next hop.

🤝
Layer 4 · TCP
Transmission Control Protocol

TCP adds reliability on top of IP. It breaks data into numbered packets, guarantees all arrive in order, and requests retransmission of any lost packets. The famous "3-way handshake": SYN → SYN-ACK → ACK.

🔒
Layer 6–7 · TLS/HTTPS
Encryption

TLS (Transport Layer Security) encrypts your data before sending. Public-key cryptography means your browser and the server can establish a shared secret without ever having met — even on an eavesdropped network.

▸ What happens in 0.2 seconds when you type google.com? ▾

1. DNS Resolution (~2ms): Browser checks its cache, then asks your OS, then your ISP's DNS resolver: "What IP is google.com?" Receives 142.250.x.x back.

2. TCP Connection (~20ms): Three-way handshake with Google's server to establish a connection.

3. TLS Handshake (~20ms): Exchanging cryptographic keys, verifying Google's certificate, establishing an encrypted tunnel.

4. HTTP Request (~1ms): "GET / HTTP/2" — browser asks for the root page.

5. Server Response (~50ms): Google's servers return HTML, CSS, JavaScript. Google runs thousands of servers; your request might be handled by a different physical machine than last time.

6. Browser Rendering (~50ms): Browser parses HTML → builds DOM tree → applies CSS → runs JavaScript → paints pixels to screen. All of this before you've finished blinking.

Chapter 08 — Learning from Data

Neural Networks:
How Machines Learn

Instead of programming rules, we show machines millions of examples and let them discover patterns. Neural networks — loosely inspired by biological neurons — learn by adjusting the strength of connections (weights) until they get predictions right.

The Perceptron · 1957
Frank Rosenblatt's Invention

The perceptron is the simplest neural network: inputs (features) are multiplied by weights, summed, and passed through an "activation function." If the sum exceeds a threshold, it fires (outputs 1). It's a single artificial neuron, inspired by how biological neurons work.

The Key Insight · 1986
Backpropagation

Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams showed how to train multi-layer networks: compute the error, then propagate blame backwards through the network (using calculus), adjusting weights slightly to reduce the error. Repeat millions of times. This is how all modern neural networks learn.

Google Brain researchers replaced recurrent networks with the attention mechanism, enabling massively parallel training. Every major AI system today — GPT, Claude, Gemini — uses this architecture.
Chapter 09 — The Attention Revolution

Transformers &
The Attention Mechanism

Before transformers, language models processed words one at a time — slow and bad at long-range context. Attention lets the model look at every word in relation to every other word simultaneously, weighing which relationships matter most for each prediction.

👀
Self-Attention
Each Word Attends to All Others

For each word, the model computes "how much should I attend to every other word?" — producing an attention score. This is how "bank" resolves its meaning from context: "river bank" vs "central bank."

🔗
Multi-Head Attention
8–96 Parallel Attention Heads

Rather than one attention pattern, transformers compute many in parallel (GPT-4 likely uses ~96 heads). Different heads learn different relationship types: syntax, coreference, semantic similarity.

📍
Positional Encoding
Word Order Matters

Since attention doesn't inherently know word order, transformers add positional encodings — sinusoidal patterns that tell the model where each token sits in the sequence. "Dog bites man" vs "Man bites dog."

▸ What is a "token" exactly? ▾

Tokens are the basic units transformers operate on. A token is roughly 3–4 characters of text on average — so "tokenization" is not simply splitting by spaces. The word "unbelievable" might be split into ["un", "believ", "able"]. Numbers, punctuation, and code have their own tokenization patterns.

Why not just use characters? Characters are too granular (sequences get very long). Why not words? Vocabulary would be enormous (hundreds of thousands of words), and rare words would never appear in training. Tokens (using algorithms like BPE — Byte Pair Encoding) balance vocabulary size against sequence length.

GPT-4 has a context window of ~128,000 tokens — roughly 96,000 words or about 320 pages of text. Claude's context window is even larger at 200,000 tokens.

▸ What are parameters? (And why does size matter?) ▾

Parameters are the learnable numbers — the weights — in a neural network. A 7-billion-parameter model has 7 billion floating-point numbers that were tuned during training. These weights collectively encode the model's "knowledge" — patterns extracted from training data.

Scale matters because larger models discover more subtle patterns. GPT-2 (2019): 1.5B params, impressive for its time. GPT-3 (2020): 175B params, suddenly showed few-shot learning. GPT-4 (2023): estimated 1.7 trillion params, multimodal, doctoral-level reasoning. The field discovered empirical "scaling laws" — as you increase parameters, data, and compute in the right ratios, capabilities reliably improve.

Chapter 10 — The Modern Era

LLMs in Action:
Temperature, Tokens & Generation

LLMs generate text one token at a time. At each step, the model produces a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary — then samples from it. The "temperature" parameter controls randomness: low temperature = conservative, high = creative/unpredictable.

Pre-Training
Next-Token Prediction

The base model trains on internet-scale text, learning to predict the next token. This alone produces a model that can write, reason, and code — though it might also reproduce harmful or unhelpful content verbatim.

👥
Fine-Tuning (RLHF)
Human Preference Learning

Human raters compare model outputs and indicate which is better. A "reward model" learns to predict human preferences. The LLM is then trained via reinforcement learning to maximize this reward — making it helpful, harmless, and honest.

🛡️
Constitutional AI
Principle-Based Alignment

Anthropic's approach for Claude: the model is given a set of principles and trained to critique its own outputs against them. This allows self-supervised alignment improvement without requiring human ratings for every output.
